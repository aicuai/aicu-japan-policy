# Creative AI Creator's Code of Conduct: Shigusa (v.2026.7)

This document organizes the theories, techniques, environments, legal considerations, ethics, guidelines, checklists, risk management, and organizational practices for creating character designs with generative AI into a practical set of creator behaviors: **Creative AI Creator Shigusa**.

**Shigusa** refers to the gestures, attitudes, bearing, and conduct that appear when a person does something. Some gestures are deliberate. Others appear unconsciously. Like the physical presence of an actor, shigusa reveals what a person values.

Since 2024, AICU has continued to update the moral guidelines that creators in the age of generative AI should value under the name **Creative AI Creator Shigusa**. We are now in a phase where it is important not to decide whether AI use is acceptable by individual feeling alone, but to build conditions where teams and organizations can make such decisions together.

Rules and governance alone do not grow creation. We give life to characters, refine works, accept human responsibility, and cultivate good culture. Let us explore the beauty of posture, attitude, and bearing for that purpose.

## What To Do

### 1. Enjoy creating

Enjoy the work. Keep the passion to make things alive. Generative AI can create anxiety and conflict, but at its best it is a tool that expands creative possibility. Try, be surprised, fail, revise, and make again. Being able to enjoy that cycle is an important strength for continuing creative work over the long term. The ability to not treat every criticism as an enemy, to let emotional attacks pass by, and to listen to constructive feedback is also part of the strength to enjoy creation.

### 2. Teach that tools have purposes

AI is a tool. Tools become necessary only when there are purposes and means. Before asking AI to make something, think about what you want to make, whom you want to reach, and why that expression is necessary. Purpose comes before tool names and model names. Being able to put into words the title, concept, identity of the work and creator, and the role of the work as a product becomes the footing and core strength of creation in the AI age.

### 3. Refine the work

Do not treat AI output as the finished work as-is. Examine it, choose, cut, add, shape, and refine it until it can stand as a work or product. The inference result of generative AI gives speed and possibility, but as a product it is at most about seventy percent complete. The final product quality is determined by human refinement. Give life to characters, adjust composition, color, expression, worldview, and the trace of story, and polish the work until it reaches the heart of the viewer.

### 4. Accept responsibility

Record the tools, models, prompts, voices, reference materials, and revision processes used, and keep them in a state that can be explained later. Recognize that the final responsibility belongs to humans. AI outputs, but it does not explain, apologize, or enter into contracts. When something is published, delivered, or shown to people, the creator is the one who accepts that judgment. Turning away from that responsibility can make a creator appear to lack integrity, and recovering trust afterward is very difficult.

### 5. Asking is a moment of embarrassment; failing to share is an organizational shame

First, listen to people. Many AI users tend to talk only about themselves. Those who do not know something should not keep that uncertainty trapped inside. Investigate what you do not know, confirm it, and, when needed, create an environment where you can consult specialists or your team. If there is no place where rumors, recent information, failed cases, and even small uneasy feelings can be shared openly, the intelligence of the organization will not grow. Specialists will spin in place, and AI adoption may become an excuse for organizational sabotage or avoidance of responsibility.

### 6. Learn organizational learning, not only machine learning

Create opportunities for everyone in the organization to learn, fail, work with their hands, and develop judgment through AI adoption. It is very good for senior members to learn AI and form small teams. At the same time, if the handwork of beginners is replaced too much by seniors or AI, opportunities to gain experience may disappear. Observe, test, and improve not only the efficiency of work, but also the efficiency of organizational learning. Sometimes divide, parallelize, or use outside organizations, while preserving places where feedback can be shared. Creating an environment where humans and organizations can learn, not only machines, is what grows future creative organizations.

### 7. Develop AI culture

AI governance alone will not solve everything, because the ways people use AI and the speed at which they learn differ according to individual skills, experience, purposes, and positions. To turn individual knowledge into organizational intelligence, developing **AI culture** is important. Value greetings, replies, and gratitude. Do not become only the person who can make things quickly. Build a culture where the next person can challenge more safely and more boldly. Turn individual successes and failures into team knowledge. Do not only fear criticism or online backlash. Understand objectively what became the ignition point and what structure amplified the problem, and use that understanding for the next judgment. For communication through AI agents, good logs, good questions, good sharing, and good retrospectives are important. Raise the intelligence of the organization.

### 8. Respect is more valuable than tokens; cultivate human harmony

Respect writers, developers, researchers, open-source contributors, team members, editors, legal professionals, clients, and the readers and fans who positively enjoy AI outputs. Something being free, publicly available, easy to use, or easy to copy does not mean it may be treated carelessly. Respect between people cannot be measured like the token price list of an AI service. It is an irreplaceable value that supports creative culture.

Do not casually use another person's work, style, character, name, face, or voice as prompt fuel or reference material. Even a small rough sketch or doodle can carry human identity and traces of creation. It is important not to treat someone's imagination or handwork as mere material.

Even if you have skills, do not criticize other people's works or production methods from above. Avoid assuming that people who do not use AI are behind the times. Hand drawing, live-action work, manual craft, and long experience in production each have their own value. The more one uses AI, the more one needs to learn from people who have worked with their hands.

In community communication, if one pursues only efficiency, sales results, time saving, and logical correctness, exchanges gradually become colder. The more one uses AI, the more one should remember warmth, passion, and humanity toward people who cannot yet use AI well, people who feel anxiety about AI, and people with different styles or techniques. Let that warmth not become coercion. Value **nukumority**, the mutual sharing of warmth.

Human-in-the-loop does not merely mean that humans remain somewhere in an AI processing pipeline. It means humans judge, take responsibility, talk with others, and accept the meaning of creation. We must not fall into a human-outside-in-the-loop state where exchanges are completed only between AIs and humans are pushed outside the circle. AI should not be used to cut off communication between people. It should be used to nurture the circle of people, that is, harmony.

## What Not To Do

For clarity, we also express these principles as things not to do. The wording is intentionally simple enough for children to understand.

### 1. Do not become a nuisance or harm to others

Creating with AI never gives permission to inconvenience or harm others. Even with new technology, one should avoid uses that damage someone's dignity, rights, safety, work, or will to create. Do not justify harmful expression or operation because it is convenient, fast, or likely to attract attention.

### 2. Do not chant danger by mixing everything together

Do not confuse those around you by mixing what is technically possible, legally permissible, contractually allowed, and socially or morally desirable. It is dangerous to think simply that something is fine because AI can make it or because the law does not explicitly prohibit it. Conversely, merely chanting that everything is dangerous while mixing different issues together does not grow judgment in the field. Break problems down, and separate what is a risk, what is a confirmation item, and what is a matter of emotion.

### 3. Do not sell cheapness as the banner

Do not mislead the public or clients with phrases such as generative AI is cheap, automatic, free, or an easy way to profit. Such language will eventually lower the value of your own work. AI is not a tool for cheapening work. It is a tool for extending ability, improving quality, and creating new value. Return the gains from efficiency not merely to price cuts, but to better planning, expression, quality, verification, and continuous learning.

### 4. Do not mistake output for a finished work

Do not assume that a beautiful result produced by AI is already a finished work. It is also dangerous to mistake a lucky shot or hallucination for your own ability. Generative AI output is the entrance to a work, not the endpoint. Without curation, selection, revision, quality control, and final refinement, a work quickly becomes rough. Do not let go of the ability to seek beauty and elevate output into a work.

### 5. Do not delegate responsibility and judgment entirely to AI

Do not publish or deliver generated works without reproducibility or accountability. Avoid pretending to understand what you do not understand. When a problem occurs, do not make human responsibility vague by saying that AI did it.

It is also dangerous to return AI inference to other humans without critical thinking. Generative AI is useful, but it does not take over judgment, nor does it hold responsibility. In contracts and negotiations, simply throwing AI output at the other party can damage trust.

Record production logs, used models, prompts, reference materials, and revision processes so they can be explained later. Creative humans think, choose, refine, and accept responsibility with courage. Consider not only what to make with AI, but also what kinds of judgments can be left behind to raise the intelligence of the community and the organization.

Creative AI Creator Shigusa (v.2026.7) is AICU's continuously updated moral guideline for creators in the age of generative AI.

Let us value ethics and law, respect others, refine works, accept human responsibility, and cultivate good communities and culture.
